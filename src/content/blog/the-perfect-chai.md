After seeing the "correct" recipe for chai from [Ankur](https://ankursethi.com/blog/the-only-correct-recipe-for-making-chai), I just _had_ to write this blogpost to correct the misinformation he's spreading. Chai is only meant to be made one way, and I was born with the great power (and responsibility) of knowing the only correct way to brew it.

The source for my overflowing confidence? The looks of surprise and happiness on my parents' and grandparents' faces after drinking the chai I made. A concoction that is approved by two generations of ancestors _has_ to be the only correct formula for making chai.

Sadly, it's time for the recipe to leave the family and be shared with the people of the Internet. In these times of stress and uncertainty, it is more important than ever to have a drink of choice that settles your nerves while giving you the agency and clarity to do what you really want to do. So let's begin.

## The Ingredients

The only choice you can have here is Brooke Bond's Taj Mahal. For a brand that's existed over 50 years, we've had multiple generations drinking chai made from the same tea leaves, every time. Even if some believe that it doesn't have the best taste or fragrance, you have to admit that the nostalgia built from having these tea leaves in the family contributes to the quality of the chai.

> Note: We're currently testing out Red Label as an option too; because The Perfect Chai wouldn't be perfect if it didn't evolve. If that works out, we might receive an update post for this recipe. :)

For milk, we tend to go with either Amul Gold (Full-Cream) Milk or Mother Dairy's Toned Milk. While the former is richer (at 8% fat) and can be used in lesser quantity, the latter (with 3% fat) is better for digestion in the long-term if you have multiple cups a day. Which you most probably will if you follow this recipe.

Now coming to the exotic part: the spices. The typical chai would stop at _adrak_ (ginger), or maybe even _laung_ (cloves). But we will go above and beyond. We're looking at spices that contribute to both the smell and the taste, without being too overpowering of an experience. Other than the above two, we'll also need _elaichi_ (green cardamom) and powdered _dalchini_ (cinnamon). The cinnamon is powerful, so we will be using very little of its powdered form.

As Ankur mentions, you don't want old _elaichi_ that has started browning up. Fresh green cardamom will give you the flavour we are looking for.

And this recipe assumes you have the usual utensils -- a gas stove (in this economy, eh?), a _chhanni_ (handheld sieve), a _patila_ (boiling pot), and so on. You actually _don't_ need a mortar and pestle for this process, just decent hand strength to break apart cardamom with your own hands.

## The Process

This recipe assumes you're making one cup (or half a mug) of chai. I do make this multiple times a day, but usually with large intervals like a sane person. The chai is only good if you take a break from it.

We start off with boiling the milk. If using Amul Gold, the milk and water can be mixed in a 50-50 ratio in proportion to how many cups you want. If using Mother Dairy's Toned Milk, that ratio can be as daring as 65-35. Start boiling the mixture at high heat as we want it to almost froth over.

While that's heating up, use a handheld grater to grate the _adrak_ in a bowl. About one handful (that's held by the fingers) is usually enough for a cup of chai. Note that grating the _adrak_ will leave some of its liquid concentrate in the bowl, which we're also supposed to use.

When the milk starts bubbling, rising and is about to froth over, dump the _adrak_ (but **not** the remaining liquid) into the mix and bring it back to low heat. As the bubbling recedes, take a demitasse spoon full of cinnamon and dump it into the pot. A demitasse spoon is basically like one of those tiny spoons you find in a _masala dabba_ in your house. Otherwise, you can consider it to be half a teaspoon.

Start slowly mixing the milk and the ingredients with the spoon. The _adrak_ and _dalchini_ are two of the most powerful ingredients in the recipe. Since they try to take over the taste the most, we're letting them form the base before we put in anything else. They will also try to fight over each other a little bit to gain dominance. You can appreciate that contained chaos for a moment while you stir the mixture.

After about 15 seconds of stirring, leave the mixture alone while you prepare the next ingredients. Take two cloves and separate the balls at the end from the rest of the clove. You will put the sticks themselves in without doing anything else. For the clove balls, try to break them in two with your nails and then drop them into the mixture. We're not crushing these in because we just want to inherit their flavour; not completely consume it.

Now we come to the tea leaves. Remove any _malai_ that has formed at the top with a spoon, and add almost a complete tablespoon of tea leaves into the mixture (proportional to how many cups of chai you're making). Since the other ingredients have already taken their space in the concoction, we need a higher concentration of _chaipatti_ to fill in the leftover gaps.

Lastly, we'll take our _elaichi_, and once again break each one in two with our own hands. Drop the whole thing in, skin and all. We'll use two _elaichis_ for every cup of chai.

Let the mixture sit for two minutes, while stirring a little bit from time to time to remove the _malai_ at the top. You should start smelling the rich texture of the chai and craving for it. Before turning the heat off, turn it up to high one last time to make the mixture bubble. This helps a lot, I swear.

Finally, when pouring into the cup with a hand sieve on top, move the pot in a circular motion as you pour (the same way you would move a teapot when pouring over a coffee filter). This will make sure to pass through as much of the brew as possible without going through residual tea leaves. Strain it as you usually would with chai. :)

At last, the chai is ready! Mixing in sugar is your personal preference, as I do it individually for family members that prefer it. This would make for a decently strong cup of chai; one you have after a good sleep or an afternoon nap.

While your chai accompaniment is a personal preference, I'd recommend trying out [Farmlite (Oats & Chocolate) Cookies](https://www.bigbasket.com/pd/40048722/sunfeast-farmlite-biscuit-cookies-oats-and-chocolate-150-g/). No sponsorship here, but the biscuits absorb the chai really well and bring in some sweetness with the chocolate. I hope you enjoy!
